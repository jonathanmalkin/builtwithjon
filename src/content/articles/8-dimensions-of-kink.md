Most kink quizzes — and most kink conversations — treat desire as a single axis. Dom or sub. Top or bottom. Vanilla or kinky.

That framing is useful shorthand. It's also incomplete. Two people can both call themselves submissive and want completely different things. Two people can both identify as kinky and share almost nothing in their actual interests.

When I built the [Kink Archetype Quiz](https://quiz.9archetypes.app), I wanted to map desire more accurately than a single scale allows. The solution was to identify the underlying dimensions — the independent threads that combine differently in different people.

There are 8 of them.

## Why dimensions instead of roles

Roles (dominant, submissive, sadist, masochist, rigger, rope bunny) are useful labels, but they're downstream of something. They describe how desire shows up in a specific context or relationship structure. The dimensions try to get at what's underneath.

Think of it this way: someone who identifies as a "rigger" probably scores high on both the Restraint dimension (the binding itself) and the Power dimension (the dynamic of holding someone in place). But some riggers are primarily drawn to the art form and the sensation of rope on skin — that's more Restraint and Sensation. Others are primarily drawn to the control dynamic — that's more Power. Same label, different underlying pattern.

The dimensions let you describe that difference.

Crucially, the 8 dimensions are measured independently. A high score on one doesn't predict your score on another. That's what makes the profile useful: it shows you which dimensions are active for you, which are muted, and how they combine.

Here's each dimension in detail.

## 1. Power and Control

This dimension measures your relationship to the exchange of authority. Dominance, submission, and everything in between.

A high score here doesn't mean dominant or submissive specifically — it means that the power dynamic itself is erotically significant to you. The frame matters. Who's in charge, who's deferred to, the conscious negotiation of authority: these carry weight.

Low scores on this dimension often go with people who find heavily structured power dynamics distracting or unappealing. They might love bondage or sensation play but want those activities without the overlay of an authority structure. That's a coherent pattern — it just means Power isn't the active ingredient for them.

This dimension is one of the most culturally visible, which means it's also the one people are most likely to have strong pre-formed opinions about. The quiz tries to surface what you actually respond to rather than what you think you're "supposed" to want.

## 2. Service and Devotion

Service is often misread as passivity. It's not.

This dimension measures the draw toward doing things for someone — toward acts of service, protocol, ritual, and the specific satisfaction of being the person who makes things happen for someone you've chosen to serve. Service Specialists tend to be highly attentive, often detail-oriented, and typically have strong opinions about doing things well.

The key word is "chosen." Service in this context is an active expression, not compliance. The difference between those two things is everything.

High scores on this dimension often coexist with high scores on Care, because both involve orienting around another person. But they show up differently: Care is about emotional holding and safety; Service is about doing, executing, attending to.

Low scores often mean that acts of service don't carry particular erotic charge — which is completely valid. For some people, service is just... service.

## 3. Care and Nurturing

This dimension captures the erotic significance of holding space. Making someone feel safe, protected, seen. The charge that runs through taking care of someone, or being taken care of.

You'll see this most visibly in caregiver/dependent dynamics (Daddy/Mommy, little, caregiver/age regressor), but the dimension is broader than any specific role vocabulary. It shows up whenever the emotional container is as significant as what happens inside it.

High scores here don't require age play or explicit caregiver framing. Some people just find that safety and protection are fundamental to what makes an encounter meaningful. The Care dimension captures that.

What distinguishes Care from Service: Service is action-oriented. Care is relationship-oriented. A high-Service person might do many acts of devotion without the specific emotional quality of holding someone. A high-Care person might do very little but create a space of profound safety. Both patterns are real.

## 4. Sensation

This is the dimension that maps most directly to the body as an instrument of exploration.

Sensation measures interest in the full range of physical experience: temperature (ice, heat, wax), texture (silk, leather, rope, skin), pressure, impact, breath. The underlying draw is toward the body as something to be explored deliberately, with attention.

People who score high on Sensation often describe being highly attuned to physical experience generally — not just in sexual contexts. They notice textures, temperatures, the way things feel. That attunement carries into kink.

This dimension can coexist with high Pain scores (both involve the body as a site of intense experience), but they measure different things. Sensation is about range and attention. Pain is about intensity and its psychological effects. Someone can be a Sensation Seeker with low Pain scores — interested in soft, varied sensory experience rather than anything that challenges their threshold.

## 5. Restraint and Bondage

Physical restriction as a source of experience. The Restraint dimension measures interest in containment: being held still, holding someone still, the aesthetics and practice of rope, cuffs, furniture, or any other form of physical limit.

High scores here don't require elaborate technical skill. Some people resonate with the restraint dimension because of rope — the art form, the ritual, the aesthetics. Others are drawn to the psychological experience of immobility. Others find that a firm grip or simple cuffs carry the same charge. The dimension captures the interest, not the specific format.

Restraint and Power often coexist (holding someone still is, among other things, an expression of control), but they're separable. There's a strand of bondage practice that's primarily aesthetic and sensory — the rope as an art medium, the tied body as a beautiful object — that's high Restraint, moderate Sensation, and doesn't necessarily require a strong Power dynamic. The dimensional model can represent that nuance.

## 6. Pain and Intensity

This is often the most misunderstood dimension.

The Pain dimension doesn't just measure tolerance for physical sensation. It measures the draw toward intensity and altered states — the endorphin response, the mental clarity that can follow a hard scene, the intimacy that comes from giving or receiving something that requires real trust.

High scores here often come with a sophisticated understanding of why pain works the way it does. People who score high on this dimension tend to think carefully about their experience: how they respond, what the arc of a scene feels like, what conditions make it safe and meaningful.

The draw isn't toward suffering. It's toward the specific psychological and physiological territory that consensual pain can access. The distinction matters.

This dimension can coexist with low scores everywhere else (a focused masochist who isn't particularly drawn to bondage, power dynamics, or service) or high scores across multiple dimensions.

## 7. Exhibition and Voyeurism

Being seen. Watching. The relational electricity between performer and observer.

The Exhibition dimension is about the significance of visibility — the charge that comes from being perceived, or from perceiving with full attention. At high scores, this isn't just a preference for having the lights on. It's a genuine draw toward the experience of being witnessed or witnessing.

This shows up in explicit exhibitionism and voyeurism, obviously. It also shows up in play that involves an audience, in the felt experience of being watched during intimacy even in private, in the way some forms of dominance and submission are fundamentally theatrical.

It's worth noting that high scores here don't require an actual audience. Some people find the charged quality of being seen satisfies something whether or not anyone else is literally present. The dimension captures the internal experience.

## 8. Primal Instinct

This is the dimension that most resists neat description — which is appropriate, because Primal play itself resists neat structure.

Primal measures the draw toward raw, body-led, instinctual experience. Wrestling, chase, the kind of play where the thinking mind steps back and something more animal comes forward. Physical contest or surrender. Connection through the body before the brain.

High scores here often come from people who find heavily scripted or protocol-heavy dynamics constraining. The draw is toward something more immediate, less legible, more physical. The script is the thing you're trying to escape.

This doesn't mean Primal scores are incompatible with high Power or high Service scores. It means that for this dimension specifically, the draw is toward a quality of experience — immediacy, spontaneity, body-first — that exists somewhat outside formal structure.

## How the dimensions produce archetypes

Your scores across all 8 dimensions create a profile. That profile maps to one of [9 archetypes](https://quiz.9archetypes.app/archetypes/): Power Player, Sensation Seeker, Care Curator, Pain Enthusiast, Bondage Lover, Exhibition Enthusiast, Service Specialist, Primal Player, or Explorer.

The archetype is the highest-signal pattern in your profile. If your scores cluster strongly around Restraint and Sensation, you're probably a Bondage Lover or Sensation Seeker. If you score evenly across many dimensions, that pattern itself is meaningful — you're likely an Explorer.

The full dimension scores are visible in your results alongside the archetype label, so you're not just getting a name. You're getting the underlying profile that produced it.

## What the dimensions can't tell you

They're not a prescription. High scores on Power don't mean you need to structure every encounter around authority dynamics. Low scores on Pain don't mean you'll never be interested. These are descriptions of where you tend to engage, not rules about what you're allowed to want.

They also don't capture everything. Relationship structures (monogamy, polyamory, hierarchy), aesthetic preferences, specific activities — the dimensional model doesn't go that granular. It maps the broad territory, not every path through it.

And they change. The profile you get today might be different in two years. That's not a flaw. It's accurate.
