If you've spent any time on kink-curious corners of the internet, you've probably taken the BDSM test. It's been around for years, and it's genuinely useful. You answer a bunch of questions and come out with percentage scores: 87% dominant, 45% submissive, 76% rope bunny, and so on across 24 or so categories.

The Kink Archetype Quiz does something different. Same territory, different philosophy.

I built the archetype quiz because I kept running into the limitations of the percentage model. Not to replace bdsmtest.org — it's a good tool — but to answer a different question. Here's an honest comparison of both approaches so you can decide which one is worth your time.

## How bdsmtest.org works

BDSMTest asks you to rate a series of statements, and it produces individual percentage scores across a list of roles and activities: dominant, submissive, switch, brat, brat tamer, master/mistress, slave, daddy/mommy, little, pet, owner, rope bunny, rigger, sadist, masochist, voyeur, exhibitionist, degrader, degradee, vanilla.

The output is a personal dashboard of scores. You might be 95% rope bunny and 12% sadist. You might be 70% dominant and 65% submissive (a classic switch pattern). You can share a link to your results.

What it does well: breadth. If you want to know where you land across a wide range of specific roles and activities, the percentage format is informative. It's also easy to interpret — higher percentage means more resonance with that category.

What it doesn't do: it treats each category as independent. A 95% rope bunny score and a 45% sensation seeker score are just two separate numbers. The model doesn't ask what underlies them, or how they relate, or what the combined pattern says about you.

## How the Kink Archetype Quiz works

The [Kink Archetype Quiz](https://quiz.9archetypes.app) starts from a different premise: that specific kink interests cluster around a smaller set of underlying dimensions.

The quiz measures 8 dimensions: power and control, service and devotion, care and nurturing, sensation, restraint and bondage, pain and intensity, exhibition and voyeurism, and primal instinct. Each question is mapped to one or more dimensions. Your responses produce a score on each dimension, and the pattern of those 8 scores maps to one of 9 archetypes.

The output is a single archetype result with supporting dimension scores. You're a Sensation Seeker or a Bondage Lover or an Explorer, and you can see the underlying profile that produced that result.

What it does well: integration. Instead of 24 separate percentages, you get a coherent picture of how your interests combine. The archetype result gives you a way to talk about your pattern, not just a list of attributes.

What it doesn't do: granular role differentiation. If you want to know specifically whether you lean more dominant or submissive, or whether you index higher on "brat" versus "slave," the archetype quiz is less specific than bdsmtest.org. Power dynamics are captured in the Power dimension, but the quiz isn't trying to sort you by role vocabulary.

## Two different questions

The honest framing is that these tools answer different questions.

**BDSMTest asks:** "How much do you resonate with each of these roles and activities?"

**The Archetype Quiz asks:** "What's the underlying pattern of what draws you in?"

Neither is more correct. They're just trying to do different things.

BDSMTest is useful if you want granular self-knowledge across a wide vocabulary of kink roles. It's also useful for comparing with a partner at the role level — you can both share your percentage profiles and look for overlap.

The archetype quiz is useful if you want a holistic description of your kink pattern, or if the percentage model has left you feeling like you got a bunch of numbers without a story. It's also built with a partner comparison feature that shows how your dimension profiles align or complement each other.

## The philosophical difference

This isn't just a format difference. It reflects a different model of how kink identity works.

BDSMTest implicitly treats kink interests as a list of traits, some of which you have more or less of. You're 80% dominant. You're 45% masochist. The model is additive — your kink identity is the sum of your percentages.

The archetype model treats kink interests as patterns — configurations that emerge from a smaller set of underlying dimensions. You're not 80% dominant and 45% masochist as two separate facts. You're someone who scores high on power dynamics and moderate on pain, and those together (combined with whatever else shows up in your profile) produce a characteristic way you tend to engage.

One model is a checklist. The other is a fingerprint.

I find the fingerprint model more interesting, which is why I built the archetype quiz. But I still have my bdsmtest.org results saved somewhere, and they told me something useful when I first took them.
